Crowd Evacuation Simulation of SFU Library

Project Objectives

Our world is a complex and ever-changing environment, making it challenging to anticipate the myriad scenarios that unfold daily. The Unity navigation system offers a valuable tool for users to craft agents that navigate through geographic spaces. This system facilitates the visualization of everyday situations, enhancing my ability to predict and prepare for unforeseen events. This crowd simulation specifically focuses on the SFU Burnaby library's first and second levels. Leveraging Unity's navigation system, I have developed a model that illustrates potential paths during emergency situations, such as gas leaks or fires, within the library premises. To create an accurate representation, I initially constructed a 3D model of the library using SFU's floor plan and Sketchup. Subsequently, I imported this model into Unity to conduct simulations. By adjusting the bake parameters within the Navigation tab, I was able to modify the paths taken in both simulations, resulting in varying outcomes. This approach offers valuable insights into emergency response and evacuation planning within the library space.

Findings

Both simulations effectively replicate the spatial layout and dynamic interactions within the two levels of the SFU library. While there are similarities and differences between the two simulations, an interesting pattern emerges. In both scenarios, I observed that agents on the first floor tend to reach the entrance more swiftly than those on the second floor. This discrepancy can be attributed to the greater complexity of pathways on the second floor due to its higher number of rooms. Despite this difference, the overall behaviors of the agents remain quite similar in both situations. Notably, the second simulation offers a more accurate representation of the building's geometry. In the first simulation, three agents on the second floor became trapped in a room's corner, whereas in the second simulation, these agents successfully escaped. Consequently, the paths taken by agents in the second simulation appear to be more efficient than those in the first. The all-path structure in both simulations consistently shows that agents on the second floor tend to gravitate towards the west-side stairs, as these stairs are the closest to the entrance compared to others. Overall, both simulations exhibit a commendable ability to replicate real-world scenarios, making them valuable tools for predicting evacuation times in the event of an emergency at the SFU library. Such insights can facilitate better preparedness and response from SFU's perspective.
